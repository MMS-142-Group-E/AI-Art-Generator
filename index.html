Definition

Extended Definition of Disruptive Technology

An innovation that significantly alters the way that consumers, industries, or businesses operate. A disruptive technology sweeps away the systems or habits it replaces because it has attributes that are recognizably superior (Smith, 2022).

Introduction


Before getting into AI Art Generators, let’s discuss what Disruptive Technologies are. Breaking it down to its root words, these are technologies that disrupt or cause major changes to the field. Although the connotation of the word disruptive may sound contrary to the very purpose of innovation, technologies and innovations were made to make changes, it just so happens that the changes some innovations changed the rules of the game. All these innovations and technologies are intended to enhance our ways of living by bringing some type of ease in how we would conventionally do certain activities (Kylliäinen, 2019). These innovations and technologies come in different forms, some of the most recent ones we’ve come to know of are the up-and-rising service innovations available online such as the various AI generators (Innolytics, What is Innovation?).


The rate at which technological advancement occurs typically surpasses the need for high-performance technology

As a result, incumbents may overserve the market by providing more advanced, feature-rich goods than customers require, creating a gap between customer demand and firm performance. This gap allows competitors to fill the void by offering products that better align with customer needs.


There are different types of innovation, including technological and business model innovations

The majority of innovations are sustaining, meaning they improve products and services along performance parameters that mainstream customers care about and that markets have valued over time. These innovations enable incumbents to sell more products to their current customers while maintaining higher margins and profitability. Disruptive innovations, on the other hand, are less common and inferior to incumbent goods on recognized performance parameters. However, they provide a unique blend of attributes that appeal to peripheral customer groups, particularly those towards the bottom of the market. These attributes may include smaller size, lower cost, greater accessibility, or enhanced convenience.


There are different types of innovation, including technological and business model innovations

As a result, investments that are unappealing to incumbents may be appealing to competitors with few or no customers and fewer competing investment options. Consequently, incumbents are frequently disinclined to develop disruptive innovations that promise lower profits, target smaller markets, and provide inferior products and services that their current customers cannot utilize.

In conclusion, disruptive innovation is a rare type of innovation that emerges at the bottom of the market and offers a new set of characteristics, distinct from existing innovations at the top of the market. Established organizations generally do not engage in developing disruptive innovations as they are viewed as unnecessary for their existing clients. Importantly, the concept of disruptiveness is relative, as a given innovation may be disruptive to one organization but sustaining to another.

Innovations are being created and developed everyday permeating different aspects of our lives. Some innovations go right under our noses with how unexpectedly useful they turn out to be, from the simplest gadget accessory such as the monopod to highly advanced AI generators that use machine learning to produce quality outputs. On the topic of AI generators, there has been a rise in the variety of content creating AI recently (Darbinyan, 2022). These content generating AI produce different types of content, from text to art, and most have been made available online for our use (Marcinuk, 2022; Ademi, 2022). With the rise of these AI, particularly art generating AI, professionals in some fields have been worrying about their careers (Pillai, 2022). With the advent of easily produced and efficiently created art, some opt to run to AI instead of human artists. Considering that AI seems to be more efficient in some aspects of work, some fear that they may be favored by businesses (Dey, 2022). But can AI Art Generators with machine learning and loads of data stand a chance against real artists with years of experience and boundless creativity?
Background

What is it? How does it work?

Digital art is continuously growing as our technology evolves quickly, thanks to new inventions and innovations created and discovered in recent times. One of the most fast-growing and controversial topics is the field of Artificial Intelligence (AI), specifically the area of artificial intelligence art or most commonly known as AI art.

According to UNICEF (2021), “AI refers to machine-based systems that can, given a set of human-defined objectives, make predictions, recommendations, or decisions that influence real or virtual environments. AI systems interact with us and act on our environment, either directly or indirectly. Often, they appear to operate autonomously, and can adapt their behavior by learning about the context.” AI-generated art can include fully autonomous works—in which researchers investigate whether AI can be creative without human intervention—and real-time human-robot collaboration. In doing so, they attempt to comprehend human-machine communication. AI is now being fully implemented in various artistic fields, including paintings, poetry, films, and music.
AI art, from the name itself, is a type of art that is procured through the use of artificial intelligence. Although there are a ton of ways to create AI art, one of the most known is through the utilization of a system called “AI Art generators” which would rely on a technique called “generation synthesis.” It operates based on a prompt or algorithms (sequence of simple and complex instructions or rules coded and defined by the person running the process) to extract data and information from analyzing artwork colors, brush strokes, shapes, and any compositional details done by human artists that these generators then would piece them together to create a new piece which is what is known as AI art.

AI art generators can have indirect effects on a wide range of industries. For instance, the proliferation of AI-generated art could potentially impact industries related to the creation, distribution, and consumption of art, such as the following: traditional art world (e.g. galleries, museums, auction houses), the design industry (e.g. graphics and media, digital product design), the entertainment industry (e.g. movies, television, video games) -- since AI art takes minimal effort than traditional and digital art, their rise is becoming popular even in the gaming industry. AI-generated art can enhance the entire graphics and overall design of video games. Thus some artists and game developers are threatened by AI art because its nature is exploitative since it draws from a vast library of uncredited source images (Gray, 2022). Likewise with the advertising industry (e.g. companies that use art to create visual advertisements), advertisers must take caution as AI generated images and generative ads, produced by AI systems from compositional details and styles of other creators, could be easily commercialized without giving proper credit.

Early AI art generators

The ‘60s and ‘70s were the times AI researchers utilized computer programs for creating more complex art, such as drawings of human faces and landscapes. These early AI art generators were restricted by the technology of the time, resulting in art that was often simplistic and lacked subtlety (Anyoha, 2020).

Improved AI art generators in the '80s and '90s

During the ‘80s and ‘90s, AI art generators began to evolve, taking advantage of advances in computer hardware and software. These new AI art generators were capable of producing more sophisticated and detailed art, some of which were even exhibited in galleries. A well-known example of AI-generated art in 1999 is the "Electric Sheep screensaver" which employed AI algorithms to generate a sequence of abstract images displayed on computers running the screensaver (Diaz-Aviles, 2019).

The availability of large amounts of data and computing power in the 21st century has allowed researchers to train and develop highly advanced AI models that can generate art and other creative outputs. With this came the rise of generative models which are a class of AI algorithms designed to generate new data similar to a given input (Karpathy, 2020).

Disruptive

How is it Disruptive?

AI art generators are disruptive in the market due to their potential to generate art that is indiscernible from human-created art. This could potentially result in a shift in the valuation of art and create new opportunities for artists who are able to produce high-quality AI-generated art.

Positive


Artificial intelligence (AI) art generators can be both seen as something useful and harmful to the art industry. Starting with its positive impacts, AI art generators can help in the draft or planning process of an artist. It can help conceptualize an idea in your mind, and can help you visualize it before working on the actual artwork. Gey (2021) argues that artificial intelligence won’t be replacing the artists themselves, but will only serve as an additional tool that can aid them in creating their artworks. He notes that many critics see AI as something that will positively disrupt the art industry, as “it enables artists to express themselves more easily.” Further elaborating on this viewpoint, he believes that if artists used AI, it can speed up their process of creating art and in turn, the quicker process can give them more profit in the long run.

Gey (2021) also points out another usage for AI art generators: art restoration. AI art generators can function and produce works by analyzing an incredibly large number of multimedia from their databases (Clarke, 2022). In the field of art restoration, old artworks by famous artists can be restored in a quicker and more accurate manner. A notable example where AI was utilized in the restoration of a famous artwork was with Rembrandt’s “Militia Company of District II under the Command of Captain Frans Banninck Cocq” also known as “The Night Watch”. In Escalante-De Mattei’s (2021) report on the subject matter, the museum that currently holds “The Night Watch”, The Rijksmuseum, has made multiple attempts to restore the artwork. On their 26th attempt, they turned to artificial intelligence to help recreate the missing segments of the mutilated painting. The “restored” version made with help from AI was soon printed and varnished, and then was displayed in the museum for three months.

Negative


With the rising popularity of accessible online artificial intelligence (AI) art generators, it has exposed more people to the idea of AI art. Although this may be a great way to have assistance with visualizing creative ideas, other people have specifically chosen to use these art generators with ill-mannered intentions. An app currently trending online, “Lensa AI,” has produced some eye-catching creations. With such distinct and interesting results coming from the application, many artists are wondering how and where is it getting “training” from? Hatmaker (2022) states that the main AI generator that runs the Lensa AI app is called “Stable Diffusion.” This software has studied more than a total of 2.3 billion images. With this huge number of media, many personal photos and artworks taken from a variety of websites have been used in the training of the AI. Whether or not they have consented to this, artists and creatives do not have the choice to have their works removed from this system.

A more specific instance that showcases the negative effects of AI art generators was when an artist named “AT” (@haruno_intro on Twitter) got their artwork stolen by a self-proclaimed AI “artist” named “Musaish.” Jiang (2022) further discussed this situation in an in-depth report: AT was live-streaming themselves digitally painting on Twitch, wherein Musaish simply took a screenshot of the almost-finished piece, inputted it into an AI art generator to help “finish” the artwork, and quickly posted it to their Twitter account many hours before AT was able to officially post the finished original artwork. Many people, along with the original artist, were soon alerted of this problem when Musaish started complaining and accusing AT of “stealing” their art, arguing that since they posted it first, AT should give them credit. Many Twitter users and artists got upset and angry over Musaish’s baseless accusations, and they deleted their account soon after.

The many issues with artificial intelligence (AI) art generators have made artists worried for the future of art and creative work. These AI art generators have the power to aid people in various ways, but this usefulness is quickly overshadowed by the people using these programs with selfish and malicious intent.

For instance, the proliferation of AI-generated art could potentially impact industries related to the creation, distribution, and consumption of art, such as the following: traditional art world (e.g. galleries, museums, auction houses), the design industry (e.g. graphics and media, digital product design), the entertainment industry (e.g. movies, television, video games) -- since AI art takes minimal effort than traditional and digital art, their rise is becoming popular even in the gaming industry. AI-generated art can enhance the entire graphics and overall design of video games. Thus some artists and game developers are threatened by AI art because its nature is exploitative since it draws from a vast library of uncredited source images (Gray, 2022). Likewise with the advertising industry (e.g. companies that use art to create visual advertisements), advertisers should take caution as AI generated images and generative ads, produced by AI systems from compositional details and styles of other creators, could be easily commercialized without giving proper credit.
Although, some ad agencies do benefit from these art generators as cost-effective and time-saving tools when conceptualizing and making mood boards for their clients, instead of gathering stock images from search engines for hours (Bonila, 2022). These industries (i.e. design, entertainment, advertising), however, also have another issue to keep in mind when using AI generators and that is the demographic representation (e.g. gender, race, age) found in resulting images. As images and art are manually fed and trained into the AI system based on keywords, critics are concerned that generated art would reflect human bias and stereotypes (Wong, 2022). Furthermore, AI art generators could also indirectly affect industries related to the underlying technology, such as the computer hardware and software industries, similar to those involved in developing and training AI systems, such as academia and research institutions.
